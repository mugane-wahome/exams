1. b. Robert Hooke
   - Robert Hooke is credited with the discovery of cells by observing cork under a microscope.

2. a. Cells
   - One of the principles of the cell theory is that all living things are composed of cells.

3. c. Rudolf Virchow
   - Rudolf Virchow proposed that all cells arise from pre-existing cells.

4. c. Scanning electron microscope
   - Detailed structures inside cells can be studied using a scanning electron microscope.

5. d. Presence of a true nucleus
   - Eukaryotic cells are characterized by the presence of a true nucleus.

6. c. Golgi apparatus
   - The Golgi apparatus is responsible for packaging and transporting proteins within the cell.

7. b. Cellular respiration
   - The primary function of mitochondria is to carry out cellular respiration.

8. d. Nucleolus
   - The nucleolus is responsible for producing ribosomes.

9. c. Controls the movement of substances in and out of the cell
   - The primary function of the cell membrane is to regulate the movement of substances.

10. b. Lose water and shrink
    - In a hypertonic solution, a cell tends to lose water and shrink.

11. b. Meiosis
    - Meiosis is the type of cell division that produces gametes.

12. b. 46
    - The diploid number of chromosomes in a human cell is typically 46.

13. a. Prophase I
    - Crossing over occurs during prophase I of meiosis.

14. c. A haploid cell and a diploid cell
    - Fertilization involves the fusion of a haploid sperm cell and a haploid egg cell.

15. d. Sexual reproduction
    - Sexual reproduction increases genetic diversity.

16. c. Gas exchange between mother and fetus
    - The placenta facilitates gas exchange between the mother and the developing fetus.

17. d. Identical twinning
    - Identical twins result from a single fertilized egg splitting into two embryos.

18. c. Ovary
    - The ovary is a female reproductive structure.

19. b. Male
    - The XY genotype represents a male.

20. a. Estrogen
    - Estrogen is responsible for the development of female secondary sexual characteristics.

21. c. Kidneys
    - The kidneys filter waste from the blood and produce urine.

22. c. Exchange gases with the environment
    - The main function of the respiratory system is to exchange gases like oxygen and carbon dioxide.

23. c. Transportation of nutrients and gases
    - The circulatory system is responsible for transporting nutrients and gases throughout the body.

24. c. Pancreas
    - The pancreas produces insulin to regulate blood sugar levels.

25. a. Brain and spinal cord
    - The central nervous system consists of the brain and spinal cord.

26. a. Carry oxygen
    - Red blood cells primarily carry oxygen.

27. d. Pituitary gland
    - The pituitary gland is often referred to as the "master gland."

28. b. Hemispheres
    - The brain is divided into two hemispheres.

29. c. Large intestine
    - The large intestine absorbs water and forms feces.

30. c. Epinephrine (adrenaline)
    - Epinephrine (adrenaline) is responsible for fight-or-flight responses.

31. c. Oak tree
    - Oak trees are autotrophic, meaning they produce their own food through photosynthesis.

32. b. Glucose, oxygen
    - In photosynthesis, plants convert carbon dioxide and water into glucose and oxygen.

33. a. Proteins
    - Enzymes in the stomach primarily break down proteins.

34. c. Cellular respiration
    - Cellular respiration converts glucose and oxygen into energy.

35. d. Adding new nucleotides to the growing DNA strand
    - DNA polymerase adds new nucleotides during DNA replication.

36. a. Hibernation
    - Hibernation is a state of dormancy used by certain animals to conserve energy.

37. c. Influenza
    - Influenza is caused by a virus.

38. d. Fighting infections
    - The lymphatic system plays a role in fighting infections.

39. b. Solvent molecules from a higher concentration to a lower concentration
    - Osmosis involves the movement of solvent molecules from a higher concentration to a lower concentration.

40. d. M phase
    - Cell division occurs during the M phase of the cell cycle.
